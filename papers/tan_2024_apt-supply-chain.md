## Advanced Persistent Threats based on Supply Chain Vulnerabilities: Challenges, Solutions and Future Directions

Zhuoran Tan, Member, IEEE, Angelos K. Marnerides, Member, IEEE, Christos Anagnostopoulos, Member, IEEE Shameem Puthiya Parambath, Member, IEEE, and Jeremy Singer

Abstract -Due to the ever increasing inter-dependency across a variety of diverse software and hardware components in ICT provisioning, supply chain vulnerabilities (SCVs) targeting such dependencies have evolved as a primary choice for malicious actors to initiate stealthy and complex cyber-attacks. The current modus operandi within the modern cyber threat spectrum is solely correlated with APTs that have shown to be quite prevalent across diversified attacks underpinning cyberwarfare, cyber terrorism and cybercrime in general. Thus, defense against such events is undoubtedly considered as a high priority on a global scale. Nonetheless, the integration of and dependence on third-party supply chain software and devices located at heterogeneous ICT ecosystems in parallel with the inability of current defense mechanisms to pinpoint nor consider enable a plethora of compromise entry points that consequently amplify APTs. Motivated by these challenges, this survey explores the state-of-the-art to (i) stratify and showcase the properties of supply chain-based APTs, (ii) elaborate on reported risks from such APTs, and, (iii) expand on existing defense methods as proposed until recently. The herein reported study aims to also relate academic research with industry practices having as a greater goal to expose this emerging issue and equip cybersecurity practitioners with the required knowledge for designing next generation APT defense mechanisms.

## I. INTRODUCTION

A CCORDING to the latest Mandiant report [1], the majority of APTs related to ransomware attacks were orchestrated via vulnerabilities in third-party supply chain software. Moreover, the Worldwide Application Security Project (OWASP) whitepaper emphasizes that Supply Chain Vulnerabilities (SCVs) attributed to third-party software dependencies have been ranked as the top 10 vulnerabilities in large language models [2]. Furthermore, the European Union Agency for Cybersecurity (ENISA) whitepaper [3] reported that twentyfour large-scale cyber-attacks during the period of 20202021 were due to SCVs. Among them, around 50% of the attacks were attributed to APT groups. Since the exposure of the SolarWinds supply chain attack in 2020 [4], there is a broader tendency to exploit SCVs. Due to the stealthy and massive impact of the supply chain [5], the supply chain-based APT has exacerbated the security situation. Hence, developing efficient and powerful defense products and their deployments has become an urgent priority.

APTs arising from SCVs are not studied extensively. The majority of existing studies focus primarily on general APTs [6]-[10], with limited consideration for supply chain exploitation. Notably, the related survey [11] on APTs fails to mention supply chain attacks. On the other hand, works that focus on supply chain attacks lack in-depth analysis of advanced exploitation techniques [5], [12]-[15]. It is important to recognize that most supply chain attacks are non-targeted, distinguishing them from targeted APTs. To the best of our knowledge, there is no comprehensive solution developed to defend against this type of APT. To address this gap, we conduct this survey and analyze the state-of-the-art.

We conduct a comprehensive literature review, encompassing both academic and grey literature [16]. The academic literature review aims to identify state-of-the-art defense methods against supply chain attacks and APT exploitation. The majority of them are from top-tier journals or conferences. In contrast, the review of grey literature directs its attention towards firsthand occurrences and practical industrial scenarios, thereby providing invaluable information. This review includes white papers, reports from reputable research labs, blogs with detailed vulnerability exploitation processes, and books published by academic institutions. These materials, based on real-world attack scenarios, provide up-to-date information and valuable insights from domain experts. Our main objective

## II. ADVANCED PERSISTENT THREATS (APTS)

In this section, we begin with the concept of the Cyber Kill Chain (CKC) to illustrate the generic steps of any attack vector. Subsequently we discuss APTs by synthesizing the tailored stages of the Cyber Kill Chain. Finally, we delve into a more detailed description of the common exploited techniques and stages during APT attacks.

## A. Cyber Kill Chain (CKC)

The concept of the 'Cyber Kill Chain' originated as a military concept, introduced by Lockheed Martin 4 in 2011. The CKC points at a series of steps that trace stages of a cyberattack from early reconnaissance stages to the exfiltration of data. The typical stages include target identification, weapon delivery, attack initiation, and ultimately, target destruction or exfiltration, which also motivates the understanding of APTs. Over the years, this concept has been extensively applied in information security and forms the basis for the widely recognized MITRE matrix. In addition, the content of it has been enriched to other variants, like the Unified Kill Chain (UKC) [17]. The UKC offers significant improvements over these scope limitations of the CKC and the time-agnostic nature of the tactics in MITRE matrix, which has been considered as the most comprehensive and popular version for distinguishing APT stages.

Despite certain common processes shared by APTs, the techniques they employ vary significantly. In complex systems,

## B. APT Decomposition

An APT is a sophisticated and targeted long-term cyberattack orchestrated by well-resourced and highly skilled adversaries [19]. Originating from military contexts, the APT concept has expanded into the realm of daily IT security society [19]. These attacks were initially directed at nationstates and related entities but have since broadened their targets to encompass private and organizational institutions as well [20]. Defending against APT attacks has become increasingly challenging due to their unique characteristics, including long-term latency, uncertain strategies, and strong evasion capabilities [21]. APT attacks exhibit salient attributes such as slow and low movement or volume, high complexity, and a multi-stage nature [6]-[8], [17], [20]. The ultimate goal is to identify numerous potential attack vectors to establish a foothold for further infiltration. Delivery : This stage involves sending the attackers' weapons to the target system. These weapons consist of remote access malware or malicious code [19], also known as payloads, which can be used in later stages to control the compromised target or conduct further exploitation. The delivery of these malicious payloads typically occurs through commonly used mediums, such as email attachments, compromised websites, or USB drives. During this stage, attackers must convert the information and vulnerabilities gathered in the previous phase of information gathering into a malicious payload. This conversion process is essential for achieving the initial foothold within the target system, referred to as 'initial access' in the MITRE framework.

Installation : In diverse delivery scenarios, the installation process of malicious payloads occurs following actions such as clicking malicious links in email attachments or visiting compromised websites. In more sophisticated cases, adversaries may employ trigger mechanisms 7 based on rare events, a typical sequence of events, or scheduling tasks [22]. The SolarWinds attack [4] serves as an example where a certain concealment period was used as the launching mechanism to evade detection by defense systems. This stage aligns with tactics specified in MITRE, encompassing execution, persistence, and privilege escalation exploitation. The first tactic, execution, involves executing the malicious code to gain unauthorized access (UA) to the target system. Subsequently, the persistence tactic comes into play, allowing the download of malicious tools or software, such as malware, to maintain

persistent control even after system reboots or the implementation of security measures. In many cases, the attacker endeavors to establish a hidden backdoor (HB) within the target organization's system to achieve persistence objectives.

Lateral Movement : After successfully infecting the device of one end user within the target institution, the attackers engage in lateral movement, which involves expanding their reach by gaining control over other machines or devices that may hold valuable structural information or sensitive data. Due to the restrictions imposed by access control lists or least privilege policies, the initially compromised account often lacks sufficient privileges. As a result, privilege escalation becomes a crucial aspect, enabling the attackers to elevate their access level by exploiting other vulnerabilities within the internal network. Furthermore, to facilitate lateral movement, the attackers may steal or obtain valid credentials, effectively performing a second reconnaissance, which corresponds to the 'discovery' tactic in the MITRE framework. With access to additional machines secured, the goal of lateral movement is firmly established, allowing the attackers to maneuver further within the target network. This phase is critical for expanding the scope of the attack and gaining access to high-value assets and sensitive information within the organization.

Exfiltration : Various APT attacks have distinct objectives, but they share a common goal: data exfiltration (DE). In this context, adversaries aim to collect sensitive or valuable information from compromised systems or network resources. The data they target may include financial data, document theft (DT), intellectual property theft (IPT), personal (ID) data, and credit card information, among others. Once the attackers have gathered the desired information, their next step involves transferring it to controlled external servers. This transfer is accomplished through the command and control (C2) method, which entails communication channels used to issue commands, exfiltrate data, and receive further instructions from the attackers. Through these channels, the stolen data is sent to the designated external servers, providing the attackers with access to the exfiltrated information for their malicious purposes.

Erase Evidence : To evade detection and tracking, advanced attackers employ various methods during the post-exploitation phase. They may choose to delete logs (DL) or remove malware artifacts (RMA) generated by their activities or simply uninstall the malware when it is no longer needed. These actions can be performed manually or automated by incorporating self-destruction mechanisms (SDM) into malicious tools [23], [24]. This stage highlights the significance of proactive and real-time detection in the early stages of APTs to mitigate potential damage. The removal of logs and malware artifacts is a primary reason why tracking APT activities after the conclusion of the full cycle becomes exceptionally challenging. The manipulations carried out during this stage may overlap with the 'impact' tactic defined in the MITRE framework, which involves actions aimed at disrupting or damaging the target environment. Consequently, containing the impact to this stage becomes essential to minimizing the overall damage caused by the APT attack.

Although we provide a series of universal stages to describe APTs, there are slight differences in practice. These differences can arise from the time spent and the difficulties encountered at each stage. The reconnaissance stage, for example, can extend over months to collect enough information for the target. The enumeration of attack vectors is a timeconsuming task, typically taking more than half of the entire penetration process. The installation stage depends on how the trigger mechanism is configured by the adversary. If the execution is not triggered, the malicious code can remain silent for an extended period. During other stages, such as lateral movement, the available exploitations can be diverse, and it takes an uncertain amount of time for attackers to exploit the proper ones to reach their goals.

Another variation lies in the potential order of stages; an attack may repeat some stages or reverse their order during exploitation. A direct example is the download of second-stage malware, which is generally a more powerful tool used for in-depth reconnaissance inside the target system. This allows attackers to find additional vulnerabilities to compromise user accounts with higher privileges in the internal network.

In conclusion, APTs can span from weeks to months, which explains why detection systems may not always locate APTs in time. Part of the reason is that the majority of current systems lack the ability to correlate data over a long period to identify malicious behaviors, primarily due to computation and storage limitations. Apart from that, the dataset regarding long-term APT activity is also not available yet, which causes the few research regarding detection of APT within a long time span.

## C. Common Techniques in APTs

The various stages typically encompass a wide array of techniques and sub-techniques, and there is no strict delineation dictating which specific techniques should correspond to a particular stage. Often, a single technique can potentially be repurposed across multiple stages. This flexibility informs our decision to detail common Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures (TTPs) predicated on the investigation of exposed APT attacks, rather than constraining specific techniques within stage descriptions, as conducted in [20].

Hidden Backdoor In the effort to maintain consistent unauthorized access to compromised systems, hackers can employ intrinsic access points, typically constituted by segments of code embedded within software or systems. These 'hidden backdoors' (HB) facilitate unauthorized access for attackers, enabling them to circumvent security protocols and remain undetected over extended periods. The HB can be deployed for the initial compromise, establishment of persistence, as well as for lateral movement or further exploitation [24]-[27].

Command and Control This refers to the strategy attackers employ to sustain communication and exert control over compromised systems, with the manipulated hosts commonly referred to as bots or zombies [28]. This technique can be utilized to issue commands, receive updates, and more. It also has the capacity to serve as a conduit for installing payloads (IPS) from external servers. This methodology can be implemented in stages, such as the initial compromise, establishment of persistence, reconnaissance, and lateral movement.

Self-Updating Mechanisms (SUM) This mechanism highlights an update process that is free from manual manipula-

tion. While certain settings or triggering conditions, such as downloading or installation, are required, the update process largely occurs automatically. This technique, often implicated in the spread of malware, ordinarily entails the substitution of legitimate updates or versions with malicious ones. It has been observed at various stages of cyber attacks, including the initial intrusion stage, the establishment of persistence step, and the evasion of detection procedure [28].

Hijack Update (HU) The hijack update attack constitutes one method that can be used to compromise the update mechanism (CUM). This tactic is characterized by the interception and manipulation of software updates for nefarious purposes [29]. Malefactors can employ this approach at various stages of a cyber attack, including the initial compromise, privilege escalation, persistence, and lateral movement.

Custom Protocols (CPS) We introduce the term CPS as an amalgamated term for a collection of interconnected techniques. This framework generally alludes to the strategies that adversaries can manipulate to facilitate undetected message exchange or payload delivery for Data Exfiltration (DE) or delivery stages. Subsumed within this classification are procedures such as encrypted Hypertext Transfer Protocol (HTTP), Traffic (EHT) [30], Custom DNS protocol (CDP), and Hypertext Transfer Protocol Secure (HTTPS) Protocol (HSP) [31]. In terms of liaising with the C2 server, malicious activities, or command execution (CE), can be effectuated through highly deceptive custom protocols, which convincingly masquerade as their legitimate counterparts.

Watering Hole (WH) This method bears semblance to a trap wherein frequently visited websites or platforms by targets are compromised [32]. The objective of attackers utilizing this technique is to exploit the trust relationship between visitors and these compromised websites. The full exploitation cycle, typically comprising four stages-selection of targeted websites, compromise of these websites, exploitation of trust, and subsequent infection or exploitation-can often be observed during both reconnaissance and post-exploitation stages.

Hijack Dynamic Link Library (DLL) : This refers to the process of substituting or altering a legitimate Dynamic Link Library (DLL) with a malicious one. DLLs are the implementation of shared libraries in the Windows system. Miscreants have exploited this to gain higher privileges for escalation and to propagate to other systems within a compromised network [33]. In some unique instances, DLL hijacking forms part of the operations for vulnerability exploitation in the system or software at the initial access stage [34].

Code Signature Spoofing (CSS) : This is a technique employed to tamper with the digital signature of software or code, rendering it seemingly legitimate or trustworthy. It can facilitate the distribution of malicious software undetected and evade security mechanisms reliant on code integrity verification. Attackers can leverage this method during initial access [35] to deliver malicious code to target systems. Moreover, this technique can successfully conceal malicious code for persistence without arousing suspicion.

Obfuscate Activities (OA) : This pertains to techniques utilized by adversaries to mask malicious activities, enhancing their evasion from detection [36], [37]. Sub-procedures can en- compass methodologies such as modifying system timestamps (MST) and code obfuscation, among others. Perpetrators can use this for reconnaissance or initial access, and for lateral movement and privilege escalation stages. Additionally, this process plays a supportive role in malware delivery and execution. There also exists a corresponding method known as anti-forensic techniques (AFT) that serves to either avoid or impede forensic analysis of the attack behavior. This is primarily deployed in latter stages of a cyber attack to erase any incriminating evidence.

Remote Command Execution (RCE) : This emphasizes the capability of an adversary to execute commands on a target system or network remotely. It enables the attacker to seize control of the target system, execute arbitrary commands to extract information, or accomplish malignant objectives. It can form part of the initial access stage following the exploitation of software vulnerabilities or potentially weakly protected customer-facing web interfaces. Furthermore, attackers can utilize this technique for lateral movement, such as executing commands on additional systems to broaden the impact. Lastly, this method can also serve roles in maintaining persistence, privilege escalation, and data exfiltration.

Network Packet Interception (NPI) : This refers to the analysis of network packets. Depending on the depth of inspection for the context, it can be divided into two sub-techniques: Deep Packet Interception (DPI) and Stateful Packet Interception (SPI) [38]. The latter focuses on tracking the state of network connections, such as Transmission Control Protocol (TCP) streams, Internet Control Message Protocol (ICMP) messages, and so on. During the reconnaissance stage, it can be employed in collecting information about the target network. For initial access, DPI can aid in identifying potential credentials or session token information. Moreover, by monitoring and analyzing internal network packets, it can assist in locating other devices for lateral movement. Also, it can support the data exfiltration stage by retrieving valuable or sensitive data from network traffic.

Wi-Fi Evil Twin (WET) : In the context of wireless attacks, WEThas emerged as both a fundamental and prevalent method used to dupe users into connecting to a rogue access point that impersonates a legitimate Wi-Fi network [39]. Typically deployed as a trap during the initial access stage, it can also be used for credential theft. Furthermore, this technique serves as a means designed for network reconnaissance.

## III. SUPPLY CHAIN COMPROMISE

The supply chain comprises a sequence of distinct products or services delineated across multiple institutions or vendors. It encompasses mechanisms of product or service delivery. Due to its diverse nature, it poses challenges to auditing, investigation, and integration. Each intermediate stage or element within the supply chain could potentially be targeted by adversaries before reaching the final consumer. As a result, supply chain-based APT attacks have seen an uptick in recent years and have become highly impactful threats. Such attacks typically possess excellent levels of concealment and persistence. To understand the occurrence of supply chain

attacks, it is essential to comprehend the components involved in the supply chain, followed by the triage of attacks targeting those components.

## A. Supply Chains

The concept of supply chains is expansive, encompassing the combination of organizations, individuals, resources, and technology involved in the production, distribution, and delivery of services to end users [3], [40]. In [41], the authors pointed out the entire life cycle of the supply chain, from the sources of products and services to integration through design, development, manufacturing, and delivery. In this project, we emphasize more on the specific subset of the CSC, which pertains to the digital network of organizations, processes, and systems involved in the creation, distribution, and management of digital products, services, and components [18].

According to the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) [42] framework, the ecosystem of CSC relationships can be simplified into two interconnected groups: technology and non-technology elements. These groups only intersect at the organization level but have distinct suppliers and buyers depending on their affiliation with technology. The NIST cybersecurity framework, proposed by the U.S. Department of Commerce, was developed to assist businesses of all sizes in understanding, managing, and mitigating their cybersecurity risks, as well as to safeguard their networks and data. It also provides guidance on managing cybersecurity within the supply chain. In the more detailed Cybersecurity Framework version 1.1 Manufacturing Profile [43], during the identify stage, the category of supply chain management is emphasized to provide clear guidance on how to navigate the associated risks. Taking into account the different categories identified in NIST's framework and other definitions, we have extracted core components to facilitate a comprehensive discussion on the CSC.

a) Software and Hardware Suppliers: An Internet-facing company invariably relies on various software and hardware. Such a company is often not capable of building everything from scratch, necessitating the incorporation of third-party devices, such as customer relationship management (CRM) systems, to manage customers. Specific companies specialize in providing these tools, systems, or hardware to meet unique needs [4], [44], [45]. Additionally, open-source software communities [16] play a pivotal role for most software suppliers. Due to their popular usage among potentially millions of end users, these suppliers become primary targets for adversaries keen on exploiting the supply chain to instigate attacks. The NIST cybersecurity framework [42] specifies in subcategory ID.BE-1 for the business environment the need to identify external upstream and downstream supply channels, which include a variety of suppliers. The supply chain-specific subcategory ID.SC-3 also underscores the enforcement of control requirements for suppliers and third-party providers.

b) Development and Integration: The entire process of software and hardware development and integration involves intricate divisions of labor, cooperative efforts, and welldefined procedures [13], [22]. It encompasses the intricate design and integration of hardware and software components. Additionally, activities such as coding, testing, and quality assurance are integral parts of the development process to ensure the achievement of desired functionality [14]. To facilitate these activities, various development and auxiliary tools are indispensable to support the entire life cycle of the software and hardware development process. Based on the exploration, the development platform has also been proven to be one of the main targets for adversaries [18], [22].

c) Distribution and Deployment: This component pertains to the delivery and deployment of software, services, or hardware to end-users, encompassing crucial activities such as installation, packaging, and activation. Throughout this process, each stage is vulnerable to the injection of malicious code (IMC). For instance, during the delivery stage, attackers may tamper with or manipulate the configuration [22]. Similarly, in the packaging stage, various components, such as code contributors and package maintainers, pose potential risks of compromise or uploading malicious code [14]. As a result, it is essential to diligently address security concerns at each stage of the delivery and deployment process to safeguard against the injection of malicious elements. Meanwhile, ensuring the integrity of the software, services, or hardware provided to end-users is also significant.

d) Service Providers: The services available in the cyber world have grown increasingly diverse in recent times, encompassing management, general IT, storage, cloud, and computation services. These services have played a crucial role in accelerating technological advancements within organizations. However, as integral components of the supply chain, they contribute to the increased complexity of networks and structures, presenting a broader attack surface for malicious actors. Addressing and securing all potential attack vectors has become an urgent and immensely challenging task. This specific concern has been recognized and incorporated as part of ID.SC-3 with high impact in relevant cybersecurity frameworks [42].

e) Supply Chain Security: Supply chain security revolves around the actions and practices taken to achieve the aim of security protection. In particular, researchers in [22] have identified the key goals of supply chain cybersecurity, which include confidentiality, integrity, authenticity, and exclusivity of components. Some other studies [16], [46] have also highlighted aspects such as integrity and authenticity. The introduced four objectives encompass all digital products and services throughout the supply chain. To accomplish these security goals, activities such as assessment and risk management, as well as the implementation of security controls, are crucial for associated suppliers, integrated software, and hardware components [43]. These measures help mitigate vulnerabilities and protect against potential threats at various stages of the supply chain, ensuring the overall security and integrity of the products and services delivered to end-users.

f) Threats and Risks: The threats in the context of CSC refer to potential attacks that can target the supply chain. According to [41], supply chain attacks are incidents where attackers exploit vulnerabilities within the product or service supply network of their targets. During the development or

build stage, adversaries may compromise the build environment (CBE) to gain unauthorized access or control over the environment used for building software or firmware. This compromise allows the attacker to inject malicious code into the software or hardware [16], leading to the infection of downstream components in the supply chain. Another common threat is the exploitation of zero-day vulnerabilities in software (ZDS) [47], [48]. Zero-day vulnerabilities refer to unknown vulnerabilities in software that are not yet patched or mitigated by the software vendor. Adversaries exploit these vulnerabilities to gain unauthorized access or compromise the software, potentially leading to significant security breaches and damage.

g) Compliance and Regulations: Organizations operating within the CSC must adhere to industry standards and best practices to effectively mitigate risks. As suggested in [41], the usage of EU and national laws can help prevent supply chain attacks. Another study in [49] focused on reproducible builds (RBs) for software supply chain security, highlighting challenges and proposing possible improvement directions. The SLSA framework 8 , a supply chain-specific framework, provides guidelines for best practices in defending against and mitigating supply chain-related threats. This framework also offers approaches to measure compliance with executive order standards within the Secure Software Development Framework (SSDF) established by NIST. Additionally, [43] mentions the subcategory ID.SC-4, which emphasizes the review and assessment of compliance with contractual obligations, indicating the importance of compliance and regulation across various stakeholders.

## B. Classification of Supply Chain Compromises

The classification of supply chain attacks is essential for deploying efficient defense approaches and detection methods. Triage methodologies have been explored by government institutions such as ENISA [3] and NIST [43]. Academic studies, such as [13] and [22], have conducted extensive enumerations of attack vectors for software and hardware, providing valuable insights for detailed classification. In the realm of industrial work, two widely recognized frameworks, the MITRE and the SLSA frameworks [16], offer analyses of categorical supply chain threats. The MITRE framework emphasizes the objects that supply chain attacks target, while the SLSA framework highlights divisions based on the supply chain life-cycle, encompassing the source, build, and package stages from producer to customer. The SLSA framework provides more detailed threat categories related to the software supply chain. By employing these classification methods, organizations can better understand and categorize the various threats they may encounter in their supply chain, enabling them to implement more targeted and effective security measures to safeguard against potential attacks. In the following context, we provide a comprehensive introduction to the two main classification methods introduced in industrial frameworks.

1) MITRE ATT&amp;CK Framework: The MITRE ATT&amp;CK framework is a comprehensive knowledge base that provides valuable information about the tactics and techniques used by adversaries during cyberattacks. It was developed and is maintained by the MITRE Corporation, a not-for-profit organization that focuses on research and development in various fields, including cybersecurity. In the subset of the supply chain compromise technique, three subcategories are given to encompass all possible types of supply chain attacks.

a) Software Dependencies and Development Tools: The development of software invariably relies on external libraries or tools aimed at accelerating the entire process and ensuring quality. However, this opens up opportunities for adversaries to compromise the developed software through their dependencies, including widely used open-source or closed-source projects [5], [13], [50]. Moreover, these popular projects may be extensively distributed to a wide consumer base, potentially affecting millions of individuals or institutions. Notable breaches have occurred in top-referenced projects [50]-[55], which are widely utilized in a vast number of software applications. For exploitation, attackers often introduce an innocuous dependency and later update the dependency to incorporate malicious behaviors. Consequently, any software that relies on the infected versions or updates can be recursively infected. Detecting such malicious activity is often challenging for general intrusion detection systems without thorough checking for dependent libraries.

b) Software Supply Chain: Within a software supply chain (SSC), adversaries may carry out manipulations on application software in several ways before it reaches the enduser. These modifications can encompass altering the source code, manipulating the update or distribution mechanism, or substituting official releases with modified versions. One of the most notable instances of such an attack is the SolarWinds supply chain attack, where a trojanized version of the Orion software was implemented [56], [57]. Some academic work has studied SSC compromise and provided valuable insights.

The study in [16] delves into the taxonomy of attacks on Open-Source Software (OSS) supply chains. The researchers illustrated potential threats across all stages of the OSS supply chain, from code contribution to package distribution. Through a systematic literature review, they conducted tree-testing on 94 real-world incidents and compiled 107 distinct attack vectors. Employing both qualitative and quantitative analyses, the researchers assessed the validity and comprehensiveness of their investigation outcomes using statistical methods and interviews with 17 domain experts. They also evaluated the efficacy and cost-effectiveness of current safeguard solutions and their usage rates. For future work, the researchers plan to investigate the prevalence of identified countermeasures in open-source projects and update the taxonomy with new attack vectors. Additionally, they intend to conduct more empirical studies to validate the taxonomy and assess its usefulness. Another aspect of their future agenda includes developing automated tools for detecting and preventing such attacks.

Similarly, a qualitative study conducted in [58] delves into the security challenges of the Open Source Software (OSS) chain. By conducting 25 in-depth interviews with experts, this

research identified inherent challenges, such as the presence of plausibly unvetted contributors, obligations to access and mitigate the impact of vulnerabilities in external components, and the necessity for more dedicated teams or tools to better audit included components. By highlighting these challenges, the study underscores the importance of contributing to the health and security of the overall software ecosystem, which can yield substantial benefits. The study employed a rigorous experimental design, utilizing a semi-structured interview approach and ensuring the recruitment of participants with many years of experience. Additionally, a stage for member checking was included, involving sharing results with interviewees to ensure the validity and accuracy of interpretations. However, as a qualitative study, the findings are based on the experiences and perceptions of a convenience sample of participants. To address this potential limitation, the researchers plan to explore the best approaches for different company types to support or engage in open-source practices. Moreover, they intend to examine how companies can enhance their development processes concerning open-source components to bolster overall security and efficiency.

The work in [49] explored the challenges of Reproducible Builds (RBs) for software supply chain security using a qualitative method. This research involved 24 semi-structured expert interviews with participants from the ReproducibleBuilds.org project. The results revealed challenges that primarily revolve around a lack of awareness and understanding, limited supportive tooling and infrastructure, and difficulties in achieving RBs due to differences in build environments. The study underscores the importance of RBs and provides recommendations on how to make RBs a commonplace practice. However, potential limitations include the generalizability of the findings, biases, and the lack of diversity in the participant selection, which may not fully represent other geographical and professional groups. Additionally, a comprehensive analysis of the technical aspects of RBs was not provided. The researchers' future plans involve conducting more empirical studies, developing tools and frameworks to automatically create RBs, and refining technical standards and best practices for RBs.

Indeed, various works have delved into different aspects of software supply chain security, each focusing on specific ecosystems and procedures. For instance, [13] hones in on the Java ecosystem, while [14] concentrates on package managers for interpreted languages. Additionally, [59] explores the security of pre-trained models stored on model hubs.

While these works address specific supply chain contexts, they collectively highlight common procedures that traverse the entire software supply chain, from source code development to the distribution of software artifacts. In our comprehensive discussion of the threat model, we thoroughly examine potential attack vectors, as detailed in Part III of Section IV, aiming to encompass diverse scenarios and challenges associated with software supply chain security. By analyzing these attack vectors, we can better comprehend the vulnerabilities and risks that may arise at various stages of the software supply chain, enabling us to devise effective countermeasures and enhance the overall security and integrity of the supply chain. In contrast to the software supply chain (SSC), the hardware supply chain operates quite differently for each case.

c) Hardware Supply Chain: In contrast to software, the hardware supply chain (HSC) involves physical components, intricate manufacturing processes, and unique logistical considerations. A prevalent method of exploitation in HSC is to insert backdoors into the targets' network through modifications in integrated hardware or firmware, granting malicious actors unauthorized privileges to control the system and conduct further malicious activities. We present some academic outcomes targeting unique hardware infrastructures.

In [46], the authors studied a potential approach to inject malicious code into software guard extensions (SGX), which serves as a method to preserve the confidentiality and integrity of applications. SGX operates as a trusted execution environment (TEE) in Intel processors 9 . The injection attack could occur between the time of building the software binary and its signing process. The core component that needs to be signed is called an enclave. In the attack scenario, the attacker utilized malware to receive enclave binary code as input and generated the signing material by assuming pre-obtained access to a machine where the signing material is generated. The malware intercepted and suspended the signing process, enabling the injection of malicious code. Consequently, a rogue enclave could be endorsed by a valid signature. This attack can corrupt the SGX binary enclave and extract sensitive information through data exfiltration. The specific exploitation methodology depends on the malicious actor's knowledge of a particular enclave functionality. To defend against this, one possible solution involves centralized mitigation by creating a secure channel between the build and signing servers. Another solution is a smart contract deployed on a blockchain infrastructure. However, both mitigation methods have drawbacks. The centralized approach may not be suitable for large enclaves due to SGX memory constraints, while the distributed solution may require additional infrastructure. The next plan involves evaluating and comparing the proposed mitigation methods.

Another example is mentioned in [60]. The authors proposed a novel attack vector within the electronics supply chain, marking the first comprehensive study of computer-aided design (CAD)-based attacks. They explored how malicious CAD tools can introduce vulnerabilities into a system-on-chip (SoC). Various exposed attack vectors exist at every stage of the design process, including high-level synthesis and logic synthesis. For instance, in the case of malicious high-level synthesis, unused clock cycles can be maliciously employed to implement a backdoor with a rare trigger mechanism, making it challenging to detect. These introduced hardware Trojans can launch stealthy malicious modifications in a design and lead to data breaches. However, the authors did not consider attacks by a malicious operating system (OS) that can modify CAD binaries, and potential malware that modifies CAD tools to launch attacks. Additionally, they did not provide solutions for these security concerns. For future research directions, it is suggested to use this work as a reference to design more

Despite variations in hardware structures among different cases, the hardware supply chain (HSC) shares similarities with software supply chain threats at each stage of its life cycle [22]. 2) Supply Chain Levels for Software Artifacts: Supply Chain Levels for Software Artifacts (SLSA) is a specialized framework that offers best practices for supply chain security, targeting both producers and customers. To the best of our knowledge, it is the most comprehensive industrial framework specifically dedicated to addressing supply chain threats. The framework emphasizes two primary security goals: integrity and availability. SLSA classifies threats into three main categories with two additional categories, as depicted in Figure 3. This classification takes into account the various components involved in the supply chain life cycle. These components comprise two subjects: the producer and the consumer, as well as three key processes: source, build, and package. Additionally, the framework considers the dependencies associated with the build process as an auxiliary part.

a) Source Threats: The source threats within the SLSA framework primarily pertain to risks occurring before the build stage, indicating a higher likelihood of insider threats. This category encompasses three specific threats, namely 'Submit Unauthorized Change', 'Compromise Source Repository', and 'Build from Modified Source'.

The 'Submit Unauthorized Change' threat involves the introduction of unauthorized changes by adversaries through the official source control management platform or interface. Such attacks do not necessarily require a higher level of privilege. In most cases, contributors with malicious intentions act as the primary attackers [14], [59]. Implementing proper checks on modified parts can help prevent malicious contributions to some extent.

The 'Compromise Source Repository' threat targets the compromise of package managers, which possess administrative privileges to control interfaces and repositories. After gaining sufficient access privileges, adversaries can directly introduce malicious code and perform other unauthorized manipulations. Preventive measures include implementing multifactor authentication (MFA) for the platform to ensure the appropriate privileges for official users [14], [55].

The 'Build from Modified Source' threat represents one of the most commonly triggered methods in the supply chain [5], [13], [16], [58]. Within this framework, the authors identify five possible unmatched locations of modified sources: unofficial forks of code, branches or tags, build steps, parameters,

and modified versions of code. An 'unofficial fork' refers to forks that are not built from official authority and may contain unauthorized changes introduced by attackers. The work by Yi [62] provides comprehensive research regarding forked projects. To mitigate this threat, the verifier side can match the provenance's source location. The same mitigation method can be applied to prevent the following three subsets. Addressing the modified version of code is a primary focus of research in supply chain-based attacks. By comparing different versions [5], the malicious parts can be identified and patched or fixed in updated versions. A general mitigation method also involves updating the related software or code to the latest version.

b) Dependencies Threats: The SLSA framework identifies the 'Use Compromised Dependency' threat as a critical vector wherein adversaries introduce malicious software or libraries during the build process. Although these dependencies are often employed to provide additional functionalities and expedite development, their benign nature is not guaranteed. SLSA further categorizes this threat into two distinct types: 'Compromised Build Dependency' and 'Run-time Dependency'. The term 'Compromised Build Dependency' refers to the injection of malicious code into the software required for building the artifact. This necessary software is typically part of the compiled source code. On the other hand, 'Run-time Dependency' pertains to the software needed to execute the artifact, often linked dynamically to run as a binary version.

To mitigate this threat, SLSA recommends verifying the provenance data of dependencies before fetching them. This process ensures the authenticity and integrity of the components used in the software supply chain, helping prevent the introduction of compromised elements into the final product.

c) Build Threats: The final main category of build threats encompasses attacks that can target any stage from the build process to the final consumer. This category is further subdivided into four subcategories: Compromise Build Process, Upload Modified Package, Compromise Package Repo, and Use Compromised Package. To provide a more detailed classification of these threats, additional divisions are made based on specific aspects of supply chain security, such as build platform, project owner, and documentation. This results in a total of four levels of threat tracks.

At Build Level One (L1), the focus is on identifying and addressing mistakes and documentation issues, utilizing provenance to manage threats effectively. Build Level Two (L2) centers on tampering that occurs after the build process, where signed provenance generated by a hosted platform can help address challenges. The highest level, Build Level Three (L3), emphasizes tampering during the build process itself, with the recommended approach to mitigate such threats being to harden the build platform. A detailed illustration of this fine division can be found in Figure 4, along with further explanations and discussions on different technologies aligned with the various build levels.

The approach of compromising the build process primarily focuses on altering the build output, either by tampering with the build process itself or by injecting false information into the provenance. During the build stage, identified as Level 3

(L3), adversaries could potentially compromise other builds, steal cryptographic secrets, or poison the build cache. Among these, compromising other builds refers to tampering with benign builds operating concurrently or subsequently. This typically involves the substitution of source files in a benign build with those from a malicious one. However, the provenance of the benign build may falsely indicate that benign source files were used. This type of attack can arise from the potential overlap of different builds operating on the same machine. Cryptographic secret theft pertains to illicit access to cryptographic secrets related to the build platform of the provenance signature. This can result in false provenance and misdirection of data source verification. Similarly, poisoning the build cache can have the same adverse effect. Post-build stage or at Level 2 (L2), there are two primary sub-threats: forging values of the provenance and compromising the project owner. The former can have a similar impact as the theft of cryptographic secrets for the provenance signature, while the latter can provide adversaries with sufficient privileges.

The threat of uploading a modified package includes four subcategories: building with an untrusted CI/CD infrastructure, uploading a package without provenance, tampering with the artifact post CI/CD process, and tampering with provenance. Two of these threats, namely, uploading a package without provenance and tampering with the artifact post CI/CD, fall under Level 1 (L1) threats, pertaining to malicious modifications of the artifact related to the package itself. Specifically, uploading a malicious package could be integrated into the build software in the absence of adequate verification and provenance, including hash checks of the package. Tampering with provenance typically occurs after an unauthorized build, where the provenance is altered to bypass security checks, classified as a Level 2 (L2) threat. The remaining threat, building with an untrusted CI/CD infrastructure, refers to the use of unofficial CI/CD pipelines not correctly constructed. The key method to counteract this type of attack is through the verification of builder provenance.

The threat of 'compromising package repo' is direct and can result in the repository's inability to serve artifacts or provenance. The final identified threat is the use of compromised packages, aiming to deceive the user into utilizing unintended packages. A prevalent method of exploitation is typosquatting [14], [16], [59], a technique also frequently employed in phishing websites or emails [63], [64]. Typosquatting capitalizes on human visual errors by replacing letters in the package name with visually similar but malicious alternatives.

In addition to the primary categories of threats, the remaining threats have been split into two main groups: availability threats and verification threats. Availability threats include the deletion of code and unavailable dependencies, both of which undermine the availability of the supply chain. Currently, there are no known effective prevention methods for these threats. Verification threats, on the other hand, pertain to those that compromise the integrity of the supply chain. In these instances, adversaries may modify original information without detection or potentially bypass existing controls in place.

2) Hidden Techniques: The term 'hidden technique' refers to a variety of strategies through which an adversary may conceal their actions. Such activities might encompass concealed communication with an external server, such as a C2 server, to either receive instructions or exfiltrate data. Additionally, to evade detection, malicious entities may simulate legitimate traffic or processes. Furthermore, they may delete relevant logs or artifacts to inhibit tracking. These collective techniques substantially contribute to the challenge of detecting APT activities, contributing to their longevity post-initial access. Communication concealment plays a particularly crucial role among these activities to accomplish malicious objectives. Given this assumption, we have collated communication techniques as a principal component of hidden techniques.

As illustrated in Figure 6, four techniques are employed with high frequency. T1573 and T1568.002, each with an occurrence of four, are prominent. T1573 pertains to the use of encrypted channels wherein adversaries encrypt C2 traffic utilizing specific encryption algorithms. These algorithms could be symmetric cryptography, such as AES or DES, or asymmetric cryptography, like RSA or DSA.

Conversely, T1568.002 refers to the utilization of domain generation algorithms (DGAs) under dynamic resolution. DGAs serve as a prevalent method in C2 communication, enabling adversaries to dynamically identify a destination domain for C2 traffic [81]. This technique boasts several advantages, including evasion of detection, hindrance of tracking, and prevention of takeover by defenders.

T1584.006 and HB, each appearing thrice, follow closely. T1584.006 signifies the compromised infrastructure of web services, which plays a vital role in phishing and C2 activities. For the C2, it provides traffic encryption or obfuscation and can perplex defenders by communicating with compromised legitimate web services. Additionally, it serves as a crucial component for launching watering hole attacks [32]. The HB technique, previously detailed in part III of section II, is utilized in communication scenarios for maintaining persistent access to the target's website by the adversary. Complementing the collection of hidden techniques sourced from blogs and whitepapers, we have also examined novel hidden behaviors reported in academic research.

A study inspired by the use of information hiding in APTs explored the potential application of steganography techniques in network communication protocol-based supply chain attacks on Industrial Control Systems (ICSs) [61]. The authors took the OPC Unified Architecture (OPC UA) as an example to experiment with attack vectors in Programmable Logic Controllers (PLCs). The OPC UA is a manufacturer-independent communication protocol with three different modes that can add additional signed integrity, authenticity, or encryption. By accessing the OPC UA response, it is possible to embed a hidden message into the source timestamp to inject commands. The study found that traditional Intrusion Detection Systems (IDS) are not capable of analyzing network traffic generated in hidden communication channels, which is an exemplary XOR-encrypted steganographic (stego) channel. Instead, they designed a pattern recognition-based anomaly detection approach with handcrafted features and a classifier. This approach can capture the network traffic created from the hidden channel with high accuracy and low false positives, which are highly dependent on the sequence size of packages. Potential trajectories for future research identified include devising more sophisticated methods to achieve closer digital distributions to stego messages and examining other protocols or channels via protocol converters.

Anovel feature set was proposed by [82] to evaluate the efficacy of privacy protection offered by encrypted Domain Name

System (DNS) schemes. They implemented a traffic analysis attack via monitoring and censoring mechanisms. Throughout their experiments, they utilized the n-gram algorithm on traffic to generate features. This algorithm facilitates the splitting of string sequences into tokens, combining groups of n tokens into a singular feature. It aids in identifying patterns in requestresponse size pairs, as well as the local order of packet size sequences. The detection procedure was regarded as a supervised problem for training purposes, and the Random Forest algorithm was enlisted as the classifier to carry out classification tasks. Their findings demonstrate that encryption methods such as DNS-over-TLS (DoT) and DNS-over-HTTPS (DoH) do not offer adequate privacy protection against domain identification through traffic analysis. Furthermore, standardized padding schemes were deemed inefficient in preserving privacy. However, the study did not take into account other DNS-related attacks for privacy inference problems, nor did it offer a comprehensive analysis of the effectiveness of the DoT and DoH protocols in safeguarding against other types of attacks. Consequently, no mitigation solutions were provided for the identified attacks.

The study [83] embarked on an exploration of a wide array of DNS applications and devices to identify vulnerabilities within the DNS resolution chain. This was achieved by introducing malicious payloads, comprising special characters such as slashes, at signs, and zero-bytes, through DNS records. The authors observed that most DNS applications generally lacked input validation and parsing context checks. The DNS records examined include Pointer Record (PTR), Sender Policy Framework (SPF), TXT records, and other available DNS record types. Furthermore, it was discerned that existing validation strategies rely on validations from lower layers rather than the applications themselves. Such vulnerabilities potentially empower attacks with the ability to inject malicious payloads and fulfill their objectives via the automatic DNS resolving process. In response to these significant challenges in open DNS applications, the researchers proposed solutions that encompass the central transparency-related principles of Internet systems development. Moreover, they suggest the integration of validation layers into these applications to improve their resilience against potential breaches.

Although various APT groups may employ differing subtechniques, a degree of commonality can be identified in techniques utilized by most APT groups. ## C. Threat Model and Assumptions

1) Threat Model: Based on our previous analysis, it can be inferred that software supply chain (SSC) remains the primary method of exploitation in the supply chain context. To ensure the comprehensiveness of the threat model, we specifically focus on SSC [13], [16] by combining general APT attack stages. Additionally, our emphasis is on opensource software, representing a more prevalent scenario in SSC [5]. Furthermore, we incorporate the software engineering lifecycle with the package manager to provide a comprehensive model [14].

The decision to exclude hardware supply chain (HSC) is based on the significant diversity of devices and infrastructures involved [22], [46], [60], making it challenging to create a single threat model that covers all HSC cases. Focusing on open-source SSC enables us to understand the goals and potential attack paths in constructing the threat model. As described in Part I of Section II, APT is defined as a targeted attack. However, the majority of supply chain attacks are non-targeted, with the main goals of widespread impact or compromising integrity [16]. In Table I in Part II of Section IV, we identify the goals of some long-term undetected APT attacks, with espionage being the predominant aim in most investigated APT activities. By integrating the techniques demonstrated in Table II and the statistical analysis in Figure 10, we present the threat model in Figure 11.

Within the threat model, the injection of malicious code serves as the initial exploitation method [16], aiming to gain unauthorized access while maintaining concealment. The introduction of this injected code to the target system depends on user actions. Various scenarios, such as library dependencies, updates, and downloads, exist [5], [13], [52], [54], [62]. For example, users may inadvertently introduce infected versions of Python libraries as dependencies, and unverified updates might automatically install malicious code. Downloads from unofficial forks can also introduce malicious commits and unpatched vulnerabilities from original repositories.

After the malicious code is inserted into the user's system, trigger mechanisms come into play to initiate its activation. These triggers can start automatically upon completion of downloads or updates. More sophisticated exploitation involves setting up specific events based on scheduling tasks, rare occurrences, or predetermined periods of concealment [4], [22]. Subsequent exploitation follows the general stages of APT activities. Triggered behaviors may involve downloading second-stage malware with enhanced functionalities [88] or establishing contact with additional services like C2 servers. Through further exploitation, the adversary may engage in reconnaissance once again to gather more internal information or conduct internal phishing. The ultimate goal is typically to acquire more credential information.

After gaining valid accounts, the malicious actor can expand their reach through lateral movement to infect more devices or machines. By obtaining administrative privilege through an admin account, the final objective is achieved by exfiltrating data to their C2 server. To avoid detection during exfiltration, adversaries may send data out in batches over multiple days and erase previous exploitation traces. Consequently, the ultimate intention of supply chain-based APT attacks is often not to compromise integrity or cause immediate damage. The widespread adoption of supply chain attacks increases the likelihood of gaining unauthorized access to targeted systems.

In this section, we present a typical threat model focusing on common usage scenarios. We deliberately exclude specific scenarios, like exploiting watering holes to compromise a single host and spreading through the supply chain, as they do not represent the overall supply chain process. Notably, our proposed threat model lacks general reconnaissance in the first stage. Additionally, comparing techniques used in general APTs with those in APTs based on the supply chain reveals significant divergence. To achieve a more unified understanding of typical APTs, we discuss a custom kill chain targeting APTs in the supply chain.

1) Attack Reconstruction: Attack reconstruction involves the application of technologies to recover attack paths or graphs by analyzing the original data sources or correlating information from alerts generated by intrusion detection systems. This process encompasses three main related technologies: provenance, causality, and correlation analysis. Through the utilization of these techniques, attack reconstruction has proven to be an effective solution for mitigating potential false predictions generated in anomaly detection [8], [109].

a) Data Provenance: Data provenance refers to the documentation of the origin, creation, and propagation process of data, encompassing its lineage and derivation [114]. Due to its ability to track the history and origin of information throughout its life cycle, data provenance has found significant use in the field of cybersecurity, particularly in distributed environments [115]. In the context of APT detection, data provenance can offer valuable insights into specific scenarios.

To address efficiency concerns associated with provenance analysis, [80] proposed a state-based framework called CONAN, which does not store historical data. This approach eliminates unnecessary phases of APT attacks and only retains approximately one in a thousand events in a database. As a result, it efficiently detects both known and unknown APT

In [10], the authors integrated data provenance into existing industrial Endpoint Detection and Response (EDR) systems to combat alert fatigue. They introduced Tactical Provenance Graphs (TPGs) to identify causal dependencies of security alerts generated by the EDR system. Furthermore, they developed a new threat scoring methodology to improve accuracy and maintained a minimally-sufficient skeleton graph to reduce storage overhead. The system overcame limitations of existing systems with cross-machine analysis, adaptive attacks, and simulated APT exercises. However, its effectiveness depends on the comprehensive and accurate alerts generated by the EDR. Future work aims to incorporate online analysis with incremental updates to TPGs and expand data sources to enhance performance.

A novel attack investigation framework, ATLAS, was proposed in [7]. This system addresses the issue of alert fatigue resulting from a large number of threat alerts. It integrates natural language processing (NLP) and machine learning (ML) techniques for data provenance analysis to distinguish attack and non-attack sequences. By employing NLP techniques such as word embedding and lemmatization, contextual event sequences are mapped to numerical vectors. The Long-Short Term Memory (LSTM) model is then trained to identify

This highlights the importance of continuous research and development efforts to address these challenges and optimize the performance of data provenance analysis in the context of APT detection.

b) Causality: Causality, as it relates to understanding cause-and-effect relationships between events, involves both causal discovery and causal inference. Causal discovery aims to infer causal relationships among variables from observational data when no existing relationships are assumed [116]. On the other hand, causal inference quantifies the influence of one variable on others, relying on prior assumptions about their relationships [117]. These concepts, rooted in probabilistic theory and statistics, play a crucial role in understanding and modeling causal relationships in various domains, including cybersecurity where they can contribute to effective APT detection and response strategies.

Causal discovery methods are diverse, encompassing various approaches to infer causal relationships among variables. Here are some categories and examples:

- Constrained-Based Methods:
- -Principles: These methods use principles of independence and conditional independence to assess statistical relations between variables.
- -Examples: PC (Peter and Clark), FCI (Fast Causal Inference), and CD-NOD (Causal Discovery with Non-Gaussian Overlapping Data) are examples

[118]. CD-NOD is particularly designed to handle heterogeneous data [119], [120].

- Score-Based Causal Discovery:
- -Principles: Involves scoring candidate causal graphs and selecting the one with the highest score.
- -Example: Greedy Equivalence Search (GES) is a typical implementation in this category [119].
- Constrained Functional Causality Model:
- -Principles: Explores cause-effect relations assuming functional relationships.
- -Examples: Post-Nonlinear Causal Model, Linear Non-Gaussian Acyclic Model [121], and Additive Noise Models are instances in this category [122], [123].
- Implicit Causal Representation Learning:
- -Examples: Generalized Independent Noise (GIN) model and Hierarchical Latent Representation Learning fall into this category [124], [125].

These methods provide different ways to approach causal discovery, each with its strengths and considerations depending on the characteristics of the data and the context of the analysis.

In the context of causal inference, graphical causal models play a crucial role in gaining clear insights into observational data and understanding interrelations [126], [127]. These models are especially valuable for representing causal relationships among variables. In APT detection, causal analysis has been applied to construct causal graphs, facilitating relationship and influencing factor analysis within the observed data. Causal graphs are graphical representations that depict the causal relationships between variables. These graphs help in understanding the cause-and-effect connections among different factors, making them a powerful tool for causal inference. By constructing causal graphs in APT detection, researchers and analysts can uncover patterns, dependencies, and potential causal links between various events or variables related to advanced persistent threats. The application of causal analysis in APT detection allows for a more nuanced understanding of the dynamics at play, potentially leading to improved detection methodologies and more effective countermeasures against sophisticated cyber threats.

The study presented in [8] focuses on developing an automatic solution for detecting and reconstructing an attack campaign, particularly those associated with APTs. The SteinerLog system is introduced as a tool to achieve real-time detection of APT campaigns by correlating cross-host attack activities using a hierarchical graph traversal approach. They developed the SteinerLog system, which employs the prize collecting Steiner tree algorithm to detect APT campaigns in real-time by correlating cross-host attack activities with hierarchical graph traversal. Risk scores are assigned based on rarity and sparsity scores of anomaly events. Temporal traversal is used to build the attack stages, and historical attack patterns and customer behaviors are incorporated to enhance detection accuracy and reduce investigation time. Despite these advancements, the study highlights several challenges faced by the SteinerLog system, including evasion attempts by sophis-

ticated threat actors, the absence of Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures (TTPs) tags in anomaly detectors, and limitations in detecting stealthy long-term activities. These challenges underscore the evolving nature of APTs and the need for adaptive and robust detection mechanisms to stay ahead of cyber adversaries.

In [7], causal graphs were constructed from recognized attack events in audit logs to address alert fatigue issues. The work in [10] extracted causal dependencies within alerts generated by EDR systems to reconstruct attacks. The authors of [32] collected causality logs from browsers' execution history to detect watering hole attacks. Additionally, the study [9] correlated provenance from individual hosts to create an enterprise-wide causal graph for locating APTs at the enterprise level. Correlation analysis is essential for fusing information from multiple devices or hosts.

However, this method faces challenges similar to those in data provenance, notably the dependence explosion [8], [9], for which no efficient solution exists. The explosion introduces bias and makes the final formed graphs challenging to analyze, or it consumes excessive resources, which is impractical in real detection scenarios.

In an effort to address cross-device tracking issues, the authors proposed the graph-based cross-device tracking framework GraphTrack in [129]. This framework leverages browsing histories among devices and models the complex interplays among IPs, domains, and devices to capture latent correlations. By employing a random walk with restart, they compute similarity scores between devices for tracking purposes. POIROT [113] integrates both causality and correlation to support semantic analysis for detecting APTs over a long time span from existing systems' alerts. By mining causality between anomalous events, the system reconstructs alert chains and utilizes Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) to model the semantic context of these chains. The LDA is a hierarchical Bayesian model that allows capturing latent attack intent and reconstructing APT scenarios. Testing on a real-world dataset yielded better precision rate compared to CONAN [80]. The system's limitations include the performance of LDA on short chain datasets. Future work focuses on real-time detection and response, implementation in other domains, and enhancing other security systems.

To detect complex watering hole attacks, which are common in the initial stage of APT attacks, the authors developed MNEMOSYNE [32], a postmortem forensic analysis engine. MNEMOSYNE accurately reconstructs, investigates, and assesses the ramifications of watering hole attacks with little modification to the browser. It collects causality logs related to browser execution and employs versioning techniques and user-level analysis to detect compromised websites and their modifications. The system surpasses detection solutions that rely on low-level semantics and offers a holistic internal forensic investigation of web-based watering hole attacks. However, it faces limitations concerning the current version of DevTools and domain-versioning technique dependence, which may reduce accuracy and potentially be orchestrated by adversaries. Additionally, investigating attacks relying on a drive-by download has limited visibility.

Although attack reconstruction technologies are prominent in the latest novel detection methods, some still rely on anomaly detection. Moreover, attack reconstruction typically filters alerts generated by anomaly detection systems.

2) Anomaly Detection: Anomaly detection is a fundamental concept employed by defenders to identify abnormal instances within original data. These anomalies can manifest as unusual data points that do not conform to the expected patterns or as outlier values that deviate significantly from the normal range. The definition of an anomaly can vary across different scenarios, thereby necessitating a diverse range of detection methods. In this study, we present a meticulous categorization of these methods, which results in the identification of four main groups.

In some scenarios, ensembling different algorithms can enhance the robustness for clustering or classification. Therefore, the future research directions focus on defence of evasion attack, expansion of the generality with other network architectures and improvement on performance.

The approach presented in [17], focusing on APT detection and addressing alert fatigue from a network direction perspective, seems promising. The network direction points at the movement of the attack activity between network zones. They proposed the APT scenario graphs derived from alerts. The algorithm to generate APT scenario graphs contains a series of sequential steps. By firstly defining the network direction inside network zones according to Network kill chain state machine (NKCSM) stages, a single APT stage is assigned to each alert and meta-alert. Then the alert graph is generated by following rules like timelines and latest infections. After that, the APT infection graph is derived by aggregating existing information and eliminating obsolete information, which can massively reduce the graph density. Finally, the APT scenario graph is created by further extracting valid paths between stages. Their work is out of state-of-the-art performance on reduction of alert volume using alert correlation and attack contextualization. Their next plans are to integrate host-level and user identify context for richer scenario graphs and also eliminate false positives.

Furthermore, attack specification designs have been integrated into other detection systems. CONAN [80] proposed the concept of atomic suspicious indicators (ASIs) and incorporated them with rules and malicious states as configuration files in their built detection system. This integration achieved near zero false positives, as explained in their research.

d) Scoring Algorithm: The scoring algorithm is a common component found in various solutions. It typically involves two types of scores: similarity scores and anomaly scores. Anomaly scores are used to aggregate anomaly or risk scores in most cases. On the other hand, similarity scores can be computed at the code-level or path-level, as explored in the work by Wang et al. [129], where they tackled the tracking problem among devices and calculated similarity scores between devices for tracking purposes. Similarly, Yi et al. [62] compared code in forked projects with their original counterparts to identify the spread of vulnerabilities using similarity score computation.

In contrast, anomaly scores find broader application. Ho et al. [109] utilized a rule-based anomaly scoring algorithm to identify anomaly paths when locating APTs during the lateral movement stage. Another study by Hassan et al. [10] introduced a threat scoring methodology that enhances accuracy and performance in APT detection. Bhattarai et al. [8] defined risk scores based on different events in various network layers, computed using rarity scores and sparsity scores of anomaly events. Additionally, intelligence-driven detection algorithms, proposed by Li et al. [21], employed risk scoring to identify anomalies based on risk thresholds.

Through differential analysis, researchers can effectively discern anomalies or irregularities that may indicate the presence of malicious code or tampering within the software's source code or executable files. The systematic examination of variations between different software versions is instrumental in enhancing the overall security and integrity of software supply chains.

The work [5] focuses on APT attack targeting supply chains via closed-source software. The researchers deployed an approach to detecting the insertion of malicious functionality in supply chain through differential analysis of binaries. The differential analysis points at the comparison of binary analysis between build versions instead of analyzing a single version. 2) Program Analysis: Program analysis in this context refers to the examination of code at the code-level, encompassing both static and dynamic analysis techniques. Static analysis involves scrutinizing the source code or program's structure without executing it. It aims to identify potential issues, vulnerabilities, or code patterns that could lead to security risks. On the other hand, dynamic analysis focuses on monitoring the program's behavior during its run-time execution. It involves observing and recording various aspects, such as function calls, memory usage, input/output operations, and interactions with external resources. By observing the program in action, dynamic analysis can provide valuable insights into its actual behavior and potential runtime vulnerabilities that may not be evident through static analysis alone. Together, these two approaches complement each other and contribute to a comprehensive understanding of the program's security posture and potential risks.

The research conducted in [13] focuses on the supply chain of the Java ecosystem, driven by the scarcity of software supply chain studies in this specific domain. The authors proposed the static analysis approaches for malicious injected OSS Java repositories. The static solution focuses on the features extracted from constant pool and bytecode instructions with intra-procedural data-flow analysis. By extracting critical information, their solution achieved promising detection rate with less amount of data to review. The detection rate is far higher than the low detection within antivirus (AV) methods. Whereas, the research has wide limitations from false positives, restricted dataset, restricted malware types, limited complexity of payload and vulnerable entropy analysis method. They are going to improve data-flow analysis with inter-procedural flows, characterise maven central ecosystem for critical API calls, and apply indicators in automatic classification approaches in anomaly detection.

In [62], the authors present an alternative approach to identifying propagated vulnerabilities in forked blockchain programs through the development of a tool called BlockScope. The analysis is based on patched code in original projects. By extracting the key statements (ks) in patch code via grep 16 function, which is a tool to search specific string inside a program or file, it is possible to find the boundary of the ks by searching surrounding lines of code. According to the similarity computation algorithm they defined, it is feasible to justify whether the new code is vulnerable or not by comparing the computed similarity score with a pre-defined

## 3) Machine Learning:

a) Active Learning: To address vulnerabilities in Open Source Software (OSS), Vasilakis et al. [15] proposed a novel technique called Active Library Learning and Regeneration (ALR) for inferring and regenerating the client-observable behavior of software components. ALR is the core technique integrated into the created system Harp which is focusing on string processing libraries. It is consist of several stages including exploration, inference and regeneration. In exploration, ALR uses increasingly sophisticated rounds of exploration to generate inputs, provides these inputs to the component, and observes the resulting outputs to infer a model of the component's behaviour as a program in a domain-specific language (DSL). The DSL can promote the inference and representation process of the library behaviours and remedy the malicious computation. During regeneration, ALR can regenerate the client-observable behaviours of the component by using the model to generate code which is equal to the original one while free of vulnerabilities. Apart from those techniques, Harp has also synthesized the static and dynamic analysis to infer the behaviour of string processing components.

Despite of above strengths, this research suffers from limitations on four aspects. Firstly, there is a concern regarding synthesis, primarily stemming from the inability to provide desirable guarantees such as infinite running time in specific cases. Another drawback is the system's domain-specificity, as it was exclusively designed for string-processing components, neglecting other types of components. Additionally, there remains a concern about performance overhead, and the evaluation has been somewhat limited. As a result, the researchers aim to extend current approaches to encompass other types of software components.

## C. Proactive Methods

The proactive method involves deploying defense technologies before the occurrence of APT attacks. This approach can be approached from distinct angles. Firstly, it begins with assessing an institution's assets and identifying potential attack vectors targeting those assets. This includes collecting and analyzing public threat data and implementing typical defense solutions. These actions fall under the umbrella of risk management, encompassing impact analysis for owned code or projects, enumeration of attack vectors, and the utilization of cyber intelligence.

Another perspective of the proactive method focuses on prevention. One of the most efficient strategies found is the application of blockchain technology to ensure the verification of official digital sources or provenance data. By leveraging blockchain's immutable and transparent nature, organizations can enhance the trustworthiness and integrity of their digital assets, thereby mitigating the risks associated with potential APT attacks.

## 1) Risk Management:

a) Impact Analysis: In [16], the number of download has been mentioned as one of the attributes to measure the impact of influence of an OSS attack. Similarly, in [59], the researchers firstly explored the subset of software supply chains with Pre-trained Model (PTM) Supply Chain. The PTMs point at models that have been trained and stored at model hubs like Hugging Face 17 , in which the popular models usually have millions of downloads. The authors synthesized techniques from traditional package ecosystem security studies and deep learning attacks to analyse potential threat models of PTM supply chain. They applied a combination of quantitative and qualitative methods to measure the potential security risks via empirical study of artifacts and risks in eight popular model hubs. As a result, they indicated that the existing defenses are insufficient for ensuring the security of PTMs. They proposed further directions including empirical study side for dependencies analysis and impact analysis of influencing maintainers, and the development of automated tool for model audit and model scanning.

From the report [40] released by anyLogistix 18 , which is a well-known supply chain software simulation company, the risks have been split into operational and disruptive risks. Once a disruption happens, its effects tend to propagate down the supply chain, which is also called ripple effect. The longterm cyber-attack is generally one type of disruptive risks. Through the simulation, optimization and data analytic against the risks, it is possible to reduce the impact by strategic planning. Another industrial solution from NIST [139] is the impact analysis tool, which measures the relative impact of potential supply chain disruptions. The users can benefit from that to identify highly impactful and interdependent nodes where focuses risk-mitigating controls can be implemented.

In [140], a risk management approach was proposed to address the APT response problem. With the goal of mitigating potential loss for an organization, this work modeled the evaluation of expected loss per unit time, limited expected state, and response resources with graphic topology structures of interactive hosts inside the organization. This model is a type of game-theoretic problem in which the organization seeks a Nash equilibrium. The authors presented a greedy algorithm for solving the game and recommended the response strategy generated by performing the algorithm. To verify the efficiency, the authors designed multiple attack/response strategies to assess the resulting potential loss. Finally, they concluded the optimized loss under various situations, considering different aspects of information like potential loss, value vector, network topology, or targeted organization.

b) Attack Vector Enumeration: To measure the impact of supply chain attacks on package managers, [14] proposed a comparative framework to qualitatively assess the functional and security features of package managers. c) Cyber Intelligence: In the talk presented at Black Hat USA 2019, titled 'Death to the IOC,' researchers from Microsoft Defense Team demonstrated their approaches to extracting Tactics, Techniques, and Procedures (TTPs) from reports and public data using name entity recognition, a type of Natural Language Processing (NLP) technology that extracts specific tokens from text-based data. By visualizing the relationships between various APTs, the extracted data contributed to optimizing the deployment of defense mechanisms. Additionally, the Indicators of Compromise (IOCs) within the data were utilized as detection clues. Other works such as [18], [8], and [141] have also integrated cyber or threat intelligence as part of data sources for detection systems.

In the project of [21], the authors utilized cyber intelligence collected from devices to locate APTs targeting the edge of the network. They developed an edge Bayesian Stackelberg game and a cyber threat intelligence (CTI) based defense strategy model for APT detection. The Stackelberg game is a theory in game theory that involves different actions on the marketing price between leader and follower firms. The authors created a dynamic resource allocation algorithm inspired by deep reinforcement learning (DRL)-based resource allocation schema, which balanced the trade-off between detection accuracy and computational resources needed. Another algorithm developed by them was the intelligence-driven real-time detection algorithm, which was based on a sliding window and threat level to generate features. Anomalies were identified through comparison with pre-set thresholds. Their work optimized the detection model and addressed the limitations of existing mechanisms, such as lack of transparency and performance in the detection model. The next step for this project is to extend to distributed collaborative defense framework under the premise of privacy. To achieve that, the primacy solution it to integrate federated learning and privacy computation technologies to establish encrypted shared defense models between edge devices and servers.

2) Block-Chain Based Design: In order to address potential attacks targeting the CSC, Bandara et al. [142] proposed a system called Let'sTrace. This system integrated blockchain, the update framework (TUF), In-Toto frameworks, and federated learning (FL) to enhance CSC security. The TUF provides protection for update systems, while the In-Toto framework ensures a secure software development life cycle. Through a hierarchical design, the authors implemented blockchain between different stakeholders and employed smart contracts among nodes to provide verification and authentication for identities and transmitted messages. The TUF and In-Toto layers further verified the integrity of updates and supply chain information. Leveraging FL, the system conducted further analysis of update information and supply chain data in a private manner. These solutions significantly reduced the attack vectors related to signing keys, compromised repositories, third-party software, and vulnerable update mechanisms. The authors' future plan involves adopting zero trust security principles to address CSC attacks.

In the work of [46], another possible solution to prevent the stealing of signatures in hardware development is the deployment of a blockchain infrastructure with smart contracts. This approach verifies trusted counterparts during integration by using encryption methods with public and private keys shared between senders and receivers.

## VII. CONCLUSION

In this survey, we focus on a special type of APT that exploits SCVs, a subject that, to the best of our knowledge, has not been thoroughly researched before. We gather real-world APT attack scenarios and analyze both industrial detection and academic defense methods to provide a comprehensive overview of the current challenges, solutions, and future directions in this domain. Employing various analysis techniques such as graph-based analysis, statistical analysis, and comparative studies, we extract valuable insights from the collected

data. To better comprehend the current threat landscape, we present a triage of supply chain-based threats based on different frameworks. Additionally, we propose a unified threat model based on actual scenarios and develop a customized detection chain. By combining hardware and software supply chain perspectives, we conduct an in-depth enumeration of attack vectors, offering guidance for defense deployment. To showcase state-of-the-art defense methodologies, we further classify defense technologies pertaining to both APTs and supply chain attacks. Finally, we identify limitations and drawbacks from existing literature, elucidating the challenges and outlining future research directions. Our intention is for this survey to benefit both industry and academia and contribute to safeguarding the Internet world against APTs exploiting SCVs.
